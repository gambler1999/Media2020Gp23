Marginal Probability Function


Definition:


Marginal distributionis also known as edge distribution or marginal distribution. The probability distribution of the components in the random vector. In the definition of the joint distribution function of (ξ, η),let $y \uparrow +\infty$, then {$n < y$}$\uparrow \Omega$.$\ $Use the lower continuity of probability to get the distribution function of ξ $$F_1(x)=P(ξ<x)={\lim\limits_{y \rightarrow+\infty}F(x,y)}$$

The marginal distribution function called ξ. Similarly, the marginal distribution function of η is $$F_2(y)=P(η<x)={\lim\limits_{x \rightarrow+\infty}F(x,y)}$$

Therefore, the numbers on the right and bottom of the table are the respective distributions of ξ and η. Therefore, it is called edge distribution. For continuous random vectors (ξ, η), in the definition of the joint distribution function, $ y \rightarrow \infty$ gives the marginal distribution function of ξ $$\int_{-∞}^{x}({\int_{∞}^{+∞}p(u,v){\rm d}u}){\rm d}v$$

Therefore, the component ξ is still continuous. There is a marginal density function $$p(x)=\int_{∞}^{+∞}p(x,y){\rm d}x$$

Similarly, the component η is also continuous, and its edge density function is $$p(y)=\int_{∞}^{+∞}p(x,y){\rm d}y$$

Example:


Consider the joint p.d.f. $ f\{x,y\} $ defined by $$f(x,y)=cxye{^(-x^2-y^2)}$$

where $ c > 0$ is a constant.

    $(a)$ Determine the constant $c$.
    $(b)$ Determine the joint d.f. of $(X, Y)$.
    $(c)$ Determine the marginal density function of $X$.

Solution

a)Since $$\int_{-∞}^{+∞}\int_{-∞}^{+∞}f(x,y){\rm d}x{\rm d}y=1$$ we have $$\int_{0}^{+∞}\int_{0}^{+∞}cxye^{(-x^2-y^2)}{\rm d}x{\rm d}y={c\over4}$$ So the function can only achieve when c = 4

b)The distribution is given by $$\int_{0}^{x}\int_{0}^{y}4uve^{(-u^2-v^2)}{\rm d}u{\rm d}v=(1-e{-x^2})(1-e{-y^2})$$

c)The function is $$f_X(x)=\int_{0}^{y}4xye^{(-x^2-y^2)}{\rm d}y=2xe^{-x^2}$$
